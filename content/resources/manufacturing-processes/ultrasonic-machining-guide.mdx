# Ultrasonic Machining (USM) & Rotary Ultrasonic Machining (RUM) Complete Guide

## Executive Summary

Ultrasonic machining (USM) and rotary ultrasonic machining (RUM) are advanced non-traditional manufacturing processes that enable precision machining of hard, brittle materials that are difficult or impossible to machine using conventional methods. By combining high-frequency vibration with abrasive particles, these processes produce complex features in ceramics, glass, composites, and other advanced materials with excellent surface finish and minimal subsurface damage.

**Core USM/RUM Principles:**

Ultrasonic machining removes material through micro-chipping and erosion:
- **Frequency Range:** 18,000-40,000 Hz (18-40 kHz) - ultrasonic vibration
- **Amplitude:** 10-50 microns (0.0004-0.002") vibration amplitude
- **Abrasive Media:** Boron carbide, silicon carbide, aluminum oxide, diamond suspended in slurry
- **Material Removal:** Mechanical impact of abrasive particles driven by ultrasonic tool
- **No Thermal Damage:** Room temperature process, no heat-affected zone
- **No Electrical Requirements:** Unlike EDM or ECM, material does not need to be conductive

**Performance Characteristics:**

| Metric | USM (Conventional) | RUM (Rotary) | Advantage |
|--------|-------------------|--------------|-----------|
| **Material Removal Rate** | 0.5-5 mm³/min | 5-50 mm³/min | RUM 5-20x faster |
| **Surface Finish** | 8-32 μin Ra (0.2-0.8 μm) | 8-32 μin Ra (0.2-0.8 μm) | Both excellent |
| **Tolerance Capability** | ±0.001-0.003" (±0.025-0.075 mm) | ±0.001-0.002" (±0.025-0.050 mm) | Both excellent |
| **Feature Complexity** | Simple shapes (holes, pockets) | Complex 3D contours | RUM more versatile |
| **Tool Wear** | Moderate | Lower (rotation distributes wear) | RUM advantage |
| **Applications** | Drilling, simple cavities | Milling, grinding, drilling | RUM broader |

**Typical Applications:**

- **Ceramics:** Alumina, zirconia, silicon nitride, silicon carbide machining
- **Glass:** Optical glass drilling, micro-features in glass
- **Composites:** Carbon fiber, fiberglass, ceramic matrix composites (CMC)
- **Semiconductors:** Silicon wafer dicing, micro-feature creation
- **Medical Devices:** Ceramic implants, glass microfluidic devices
- **Aerospace:** CMC turbine components, ceramic bearings
- **Electronics:** Piezoelectric ceramics, ferrites, sapphire substrates

**Economic Benefits:**

When to choose ultrasonic machining:
- **Hard Materials (>Rc 55):** Conventional machining extremely slow or impossible
- **Brittle Materials:** Ceramics and glass crack with conventional machining forces
- **Precision Requirements:** Tight tolerances with excellent surface finish
- **Subsurface Integrity:** No microcracks or heat damage acceptable
- **Small Features:** Holes <0.050" diameter, thin walls, intricate details

## Understanding Ultrasonic Machining Fundamentals

### USM Process Mechanism

**Material Removal Process:**

Unlike conventional machining that removes material through plastic deformation and shearing, USM removes material through:

1. **Abrasive Impact:** Tool vibrates at 20-40 kHz, impacting abrasive particles
2. **Micro-Chipping:** Abrasive particles impact workpiece surface, creating micro-fractures
3. **Erosion:** Repeated impacts erode material grain-by-grain
4. **Flushing:** Slurry carries away debris and brings fresh abrasive

**Key Process Components:**

**1. Ultrasonic Transducer:**

Converts electrical energy to mechanical vibration:
- **Type:** Piezoelectric (most common) or magnetostrictive
- **Frequency:** 18-40 kHz (beyond human hearing range)
- **Power:** 50-3000 watts depending on application
- **Efficiency:** 90-95% electrical to mechanical conversion

**Piezoelectric Effect:**
- Piezoelectric ceramics (lead zirconate titanate - PZT) expand/contract with applied voltage
- Alternating current at resonant frequency creates vibration
- Vibration amplitude amplified through mechanical horn/concentrator

**2. Horn/Concentrator:**

Amplifies and focuses ultrasonic vibration:
- **Material:** Titanium alloy (most common), steel, aluminum
- **Function:** Amplify vibration amplitude from 2-5 μm to 10-50 μm
- **Design:** Tapered geometry for mechanical amplification
- **Resonance:** Tuned to match transducer frequency

**Amplitude Calculation:**
Amplitude Gain = (Input Area / Output Area)^0.5

**Example:**
- Input diameter: 2.0"
- Output diameter: 0.5"
- Amplitude gain: (4.0 / 0.25)^0.5 = 4.0x
- Input amplitude: 10 μm
- Output amplitude: 40 μm

**3. Tool (Sonotrode):**

The cutting element that contacts workpiece:
- **Material:** Soft steel (for drilling), copper, brass, or stainless
- **Shape:** Negative of desired feature (drill, cavity shape, profile)
- **Wear:** Tool wears during process, periodic replacement needed
- **Design:** Undersize to account for gap and overcut from abrasive

**Tool Undersize Calculation:**
Tool Diameter = Hole Diameter - 2 × (Amplitude + Abrasive Gap)

**Example for 0.250" Hole:**
- Desired hole: 0.250"
- Vibration amplitude: 0.002" (50 μm)
- Abrasive gap: 0.001" (25 μm)
- Tool diameter: 0.250 - 2(0.002 + 0.001) = 0.244"

**4. Abrasive Slurry:**

Carries abrasive particles between tool and workpiece:

**Slurry Components:**
- **Abrasive Particles:** Boron carbide, silicon carbide, aluminum oxide, diamond
- **Carrier Fluid:** Water (most common), oil, synthetic coolant
- **Concentration:** 20-50% abrasive by volume
- **Viscosity:** Controlled for optimal particle suspension and flow

**Abrasive Selection by Material:**

| Workpiece Material | Recommended Abrasive | Grit Size | Concentration |
|--------------------|---------------------|-----------|---------------|
| **Glass** | Boron carbide | 180-400 mesh | 30-40% |
| **Alumina Ceramic** | Boron carbide | 120-320 mesh | 30-50% |
| **Silicon Carbide Ceramic** | Boron carbide or diamond | 180-400 mesh | 30-40% |
| **Zirconia Ceramic** | Diamond | 200-500 mesh | 25-35% |
| **Quartz** | Silicon carbide | 180-320 mesh | 30-40% |

| **Graphite Composites** | Aluminum oxide or SiC | 120-240 mesh | 30-40% |

**Grit Size Effects:**

| Grit Size | Particle Size | Material Removal Rate | Surface Finish | Application |
|-----------|---------------|----------------------|----------------|-------------|
| **60-120 mesh** | 125-250 μm | High | Rough (32-63 μin Ra) | Roughing, high MRR |
| **120-240 mesh** | 63-125 μm | Moderate | Medium (16-32 μin Ra) | General purpose |
| **240-400 mesh** | 38-63 μm | Low-moderate | Good (8-16 μin Ra) | Finishing |
| **400-800 mesh** | 18-38 μm | Low | Excellent (4-8 μin Ra) | Fine finishing, optics |

**5. Downward Force:**

Static force pressing tool into workpiece:
- **Typical Force:** 5-50 pounds (2-22 kg) depending on tool size
- **Too Low:** Inefficient material removal, tool bounces
- **Too High:** Excessive tool wear, possible workpiece damage
- **Optimal:** Just enough to maintain contact while allowing vibration

### Material Removal Rate Fundamentals

**MRR Equation:**

**MRR = k × f × A × P**

Where:
- MRR = Material Removal Rate (mm³/min or in³/min)
- k = Material constant (depends on hardness, brittleness)
- f = Frequency (Hz)
- A = Amplitude (μm or inches)
- P = Static pressure (force / tool area)

**Typical Material Removal Rates (USM):**

| Material | Hardness | MRR (mm³/min) | MRR (in³/min) | Drilling Rate (mm/min) |
|----------|----------|---------------|---------------|----------------------|
| **Glass (Soda Lime)** | 5.5 Mohs | 2-8 | 0.00012-0.00049 | 1.5-3.0 |
| **Glass (Borosilicate)** | 6.0 Mohs | 1.5-6 | 0.00009-0.00037 | 1.0-2.5 |
| **Alumina (Al₂O₃) 96%** | 9.0 Mohs | 0.8-4 | 0.00005-0.00024 | 0.5-2.0 |
| **Zirconia (ZrO₂)** | 8.5 Mohs | 1.0-5 | 0.00006-0.00031 | 0.8-2.5 |
| **Silicon Carbide (SiC)** | 9.5 Mohs | 0.5-2.5 | 0.00003-0.00015 | 0.3-1.5 |
| **Silicon Nitride (Si₃N₄)** | 9.0 Mohs | 0.6-3 | 0.00004-0.00018 | 0.4-1.8 |
| **Sapphire (Al₂O₃)** | 9.0 Mohs | 0.3-1.5 | 0.00002-0.00009 | 0.2-1.0 |
| **Tungsten Carbide** | 9.0 Mohs | 0.5-3 | 0.00003-0.00018 | 0.3-1.5 |
| **Quartz** | 7.0 Mohs | 1.5-6 | 0.00009-0.00037 | 1.0-3.0 |

**Hole Drilling Example:**

**Drill 0.100" diameter hole, 0.250" deep in alumina ceramic:**
- Material removal rate: 1.5 mm³/min (average)
- Hole volume: π × (1.27 mm)² × 6.35 mm = 32.1 mm³
- **Drilling time: 32.1 / 1.5 = 21.4 minutes**

**Factors Affecting MRR:**

| Factor | Effect on MRR | Optimal Range | Notes |
|--------|---------------|---------------|-------|
| **Frequency** | Higher = faster (up to limit) | 20-25 kHz for general work | >30 kHz for micro-machining |
| **Amplitude** | Higher = faster, but more wear | 30-50 μm for general work | 10-25 μm for fine features |
| **Abrasive Grit** | Coarser = faster, rougher | 180-240 mesh general purpose | 400+ mesh for finishing |
| **Slurry Concentration** | Optimal at 30-40% | 30-40% by volume | Too low: slow; too high: flow issues |
| **Static Force** | Higher = faster (up to limit) | 0.5-2.0 psi tool face pressure | Excessive force damages workpiece |
| **Tool Material** | Softer tool = faster (wears faster) | Steel for rough, brass for finish | Copper for very soft ceramics |

## Rotary Ultrasonic Machining (RUM)

### RUM Process Description

**Innovation Over Conventional USM:**

RUM combines ultrasonic vibration with tool rotation:
- **Ultrasonic Vibration:** 20-40 kHz in axial direction (toward workpiece)
- **Rotation:** 500-8,000 RPM tool rotation
- **Result:** Distributed tool wear, higher MRR, ability to cut complex contours

**RUM Advantages:**

| Aspect | Conventional USM | Rotary USM (RUM) | Advantage |
|--------|-----------------|-----------------|-----------|
| **Tool Wear** | Concentrated at tool face | Distributed around periphery | RUM: 2-5x longer tool life |
| **Material Removal Rate** | Limited by single impact zone | Multiple cutting edges | RUM: 5-20x higher MRR |
| **Feature Types** | Holes, simple pockets | Complex 3D milling, grinding | RUM: much more versatile |
| **Surface Finish** | 8-32 μin Ra | 8-32 μin Ra | Comparable |
| **Equipment Cost** | Lower ($20k-100k) | Higher ($100k-500k+) | USM lower investment |
| **Tool Cost** | Lower (simple tools) | Higher (diamond core drills, grinding wheels) | USM lower consumables |

### RUM Equipment and Tooling

**RUM Machine Components:**

**1. Spindle:**
- **Type:** High-frequency spindle with integrated ultrasonic transducer
- **Speed Range:** 500-8,000 RPM typical
- **Power:** 1-10 kW spindle motor
- **Ultrasonic Power:** 100-2,000 watts ultrasonic

**2. Cutting Tools:**

**Diamond Core Drills (Most Common):**
- **Construction:** Metal bond diamond on tubular steel core
- **Diamond Grit:** 60-400 grit depending on application
- **Diameter Range:** 0.020" to 4.000" (0.5-100 mm)
- **Applications:** Hole drilling in ceramics, glass, composites

**Diamond Grinding Wheels:**
- **Types:** Cup wheels, straight wheels, profile wheels
- **Bond:** Electroplated or metal-bonded diamond
- **Grit:** 80-600 grit
- **Applications:** Surface grinding, profile grinding

**End Mill Style Tools:**
- **Construction:** Diamond-coated or diamond-impregnated
- **Geometry:** Similar to carbide end mills
- **Applications:** Slotting, contouring, 3D milling

**3. Coolant/Slurry System:**

Unlike conventional USM that requires abrasive slurry, RUM typically uses:
- **Water-Based Coolant:** For cooling and chip removal
- **Diamond Tool Abrasive:** Bonded to tool, not in slurry
- **Flow Rate:** 1-10 GPM depending on tool size
- **Pressure:** 50-150 PSI for effective chip evacuation

**Feed Rate (mm/min) = Feed per Rev (mm/rev) × RPM**

**Example:**
- Feed per revolution: 0.003 mm/rev (recommended for alumina)
- Spindle speed: 3000 RPM
- **Feed rate = 0.003 × 3000 = 9 mm/min**

### RUM vs. Conventional Machining

**Performance Comparison: Drilling 0.250" Hole, 0.500" Deep in Zirconia**

| Process | Cycle Time | Tool Cost per Hole | Surface Finish | Edge Quality | Total Cost per Hole |
|---------|-----------|-------------------|----------------|--------------|---------------------|

| **Conventional USM** | 30-60 minutes | $0.10-0.25 | 16 μin Ra | Excellent, no chipping | $28-55 |
| **Diamond Drill (No US)** | 8-15 minutes | $1.50-3.00 | 20 μin Ra | Fair, some edge chipping | $10-18 |
| **Carbide Drill** | Not feasible | Tool fractures | N/A | Severe chipping/cracking | N/A |
| **Laser Drilling** | 1-3 minutes | $0.50-1.50 | 40 μin Ra | Fair, heat-affected zone | $4-8 |

**When RUM is Superior:**
- Hard, brittle materials (ceramics, glass, sapphire)
- Tight tolerances required (±0.001")
- Excellent surface finish needed (8-32 μin Ra)
- No subsurface damage acceptable (medical, optical)
- Complex features (not just simple holes)

**When Other Processes May Be Better:**
- **Laser:** Very small features (<0.020" dia), high volume, surface integrity less critical
- **Conventional Drill:** Machinable ceramics (macor, alumina <90%), lower tolerance requirements
- **EDM:** Electrically conductive ceramics (tungsten carbide), complex 3D cavities
- **Water Jet:** Large features (>0.125"), through-cutting, lower tolerance requirements

## Applications by Industry

### Medical Device Manufacturing

**Orthopedic Ceramic Implants:**

**Zirconia and Alumina Hip/Knee Components:**
- **Applications:** Femoral heads, acetabular cups, bearing surfaces
- **Materials:** Yttria-stabilized zirconia (Y-TZP), high-purity alumina
- **Features:** Spherical surfaces, mounting holes, thread tapping holes
- **Tolerances:** ±0.001-0.002" on critical bearing surfaces
- **Surface Finish:** 4-8 μin Ra for bearing surfaces (low friction, wear resistance)

**RUM Process:**
- Surface grinding: 1000-3000 RPM, fine diamond wheel (400-600 grit)
- Hole drilling: 2000-4000 RPM, diamond core drills
- Finish: Two-stage grinding (coarse 200 grit → fine 600 grit)
- Quality: CMM inspection, profilometer surface finish verification

**Cost Advantage:**
- RUM: $50-150 per implant component (machining)
- Conventional grinding: $200-500 per component (much slower)
- Productivity: 3-5x improvement with RUM

**Surgical Instrument Components:**

**Ceramic Cutting Edges and Inserts:**
- **Material:** Alumina, zirconia (for scissors, knives, scalpels)
- **Features:** Sharp edges (0.002-0.005" radius), precision holes
- **Advantage:** Ceramic maintains sharpness 10x longer than steel

**Microfluidic Devices:**

**Glass Microchannels:**
- **Material:** Borosilicate glass, fused silica
- **Features:** Channels 0.010-0.100" wide, depths 0.005-0.050"
- **Tolerances:** ±0.0005" on channel dimensions
- **Applications:** Lab-on-a-chip devices, diagnostic testing

**USM Micro-Machining:**
- Frequency: 35-40 kHz (high frequency for small features)
- Tools: Custom micro-tools 0.005-0.050" diameter
- Abrasive: 400-800 grit boron carbide for fine features
- Surface finish: 4-8 μin Ra for smooth fluid flow

### Aerospace Industry

**Ceramic Matrix Composite (CMC) Components:**

**Turbine Engine Hot Section Parts:**
- **Material:** SiC/SiC ceramic matrix composite

**RUM Advantages for CMC:**
- No fiber pullout or delamination (vs. conventional machining)

**Ceramic Bearings:**

**Silicon Nitride (Si₃N₄) Ball Bearings:**
- **Applications:** High-temperature bearings, aerospace, high-speed machinery

- Two-stage process: rough grind (220 grit) → finish grind (600 grit)

### Semiconductor and Electronics

**Silicon Wafer Processing:**

**Wafer Dicing and Micro-Feature Creation:**
- **Material:** Silicon wafers (100-300mm diameter, 0.5-1.0mm thick)
- **Applications:** Die separation, through-silicon vias (TSV), micro-channels
- **Challenge:** Minimize chipping and cracking

**USM Dicing:**
- Frequency: 35-40 kHz
- Tool: Diamond blade 0.010-0.030" thick
- Feed rate: 10-50 mm/min
- Advantage: 80% less edge chipping vs. conventional dicing saw

**Sapphire Substrate Machining:**

**LED and Power Electronics Substrates:**
- **Material:** Single-crystal sapphire (extremely hard, Mohs 9)
- **Features:** Holes for vias, cavities for chips, scribing for breaking
- **Conventional Machining:** Nearly impossible (extreme hardness)

**RUM for Sapphire:**
- Drilling: 1500-2500 RPM, 1-4 mm/min, diamond core drill
- Scribing: Diamond wheel, 0.1-0.3 mm depth, precise control
- Result: Clean holes, minimal subsurface damage, 12-16 μin Ra

**Piezoelectric Ceramic Processing:**

**PZT (Lead Zirconate Titanate) Components:**
- **Applications:** Ultrasonic transducers, sensors, actuators
- **Features:** Thin walls (0.010-0.050"), precise holes, complex shapes
- **Challenge:** Brittle, cracks easily with conventional machining

**USM Advantages:**
- No heat generation (preserves piezoelectric properties)
- Low stress (prevents cracking)
- Excellent surface finish (minimizes stress concentrations)

## Process Planning and Optimization

### Selecting USM vs. RUM

**Decision Matrix:**

| Factor | Choose Conventional USM | Choose RUM |
|--------|------------------------|------------|
| **Feature Type** | Simple holes, blind pockets | Complex 3D contours, milling |
| **Production Volume** | Low volume (<100 parts/year) | Medium-high volume (>100 parts/year) |
| **Material Removal** | Low (<1 cm³ per feature) | High (>1 cm³ per feature) |
| **Equipment Budget** | Limited ($20k-100k) | Adequate ($100k-500k+) |
| **Tool Cost Sensitivity** | Prefer low tool cost | Higher tool cost acceptable for productivity |
| **Skill Level Available** | Basic operator training | CNC programming capability |
| **Lead Time** | Less critical (low volume) | Critical (production volumes) |

### Tooling Design

**USM Tool Design Guidelines:**

**1. Tool Undersize for Overcut:**

Account for amplitude and abrasive gap:
- **Radial Clearance:** (Amplitude + Abrasive Particle Size) per side
- **Example:** For 40 μm amplitude, 30 μm abrasive, clearance = 70 μm per side

**2. Tool Material Selection:**

| Tool Material | Hardness | Wear Resistance | MRR | Best Application |
|---------------|----------|----------------|-----|------------------|
| **Soft Steel (1018)** | Low | Low | Highest | Roughing, maximum MRR, short runs |
| **Tool Steel (O1, A2)** | Medium | Medium | Medium | General purpose, moderate runs |
| **Stainless Steel** | Medium | Medium-high | Medium | Corrosion resistance needed |
| **Brass** | Low | Low | High | Softer ceramics, good surface finish |
| **Copper** | Very low | Very low | Very high | Very soft ceramics, short runs |

**Tool Wear and Life:**

**Typical Tool Life (Volume Removed Before Replacement):**

| Workpiece Material | Tool Material | Volume Before Replacement | Replacement Indicator |
|--------------------|---------------|--------------------------|---------------------|
| **Glass** | Soft steel | 5-15 cm³ | 10-20% dimensional growth |
| **Alumina** | Tool steel | 3-10 cm³ | 15-25% dimensional growth |
| **Silicon Carbide** | Hardened steel | 1-5 cm³ | 20-30% dimensional growth |
| **Sapphire** | Hardened steel | 0.5-2 cm³ | 25-35% dimensional growth |

**Tool Replacement Strategy:**
- Monitor dimensional growth with periodic measurement
- Replace when feature dimension grows beyond tolerance
- For production, establish replacement interval based on volume
- First article inspection after each tool change

### Quality Control and Inspection

**Dimensional Inspection:**

**Challenges:**
- Brittle materials crack easily if clamped improperly
- Surface finish affects measurement (optical methods)
- Internal features difficult to access

**Inspection Methods:**

| Feature Type | Measurement Method | Accuracy | Notes |
|--------------|-------------------|----------|-------|
| **Hole Diameter** | Pin gauges, air gauging | ±0.0001-0.0005" | Air gauging best for small holes |
| **Hole Depth** | Depth micrometer, optical | ±0.001-0.002" | Through holes: measure total thickness |
| **Surface Finish** | Profilometer (stylus or optical) | ±2 μin Ra | Optical for very smooth surfaces |
| **Flatness** | Optical flat, interferometer | ±0.000020" | Interferometry for precision surfaces |
| **Complex 3D** | CMM, optical scanning | ±0.001-0.003" | Optical scanning for full surface |

**Subsurface Damage Assessment:**

Critical for optical, medical, and structural applications:

**Methods:**
1. **Optical Microscopy:** Surface cracks, chipping (100-1000x magnification)
2. **SEM (Scanning Electron Microscope):** Micro-crack detection, surface morphology
3. **Cross-Section Metallography:** Grind, polish, examine subsurface layers
4. **Dye Penetrant:** Surface crack detection

**Acceptance Criteria:**

| Application | Surface Cracks | Subsurface Damage Depth | Chipping |
|-------------|----------------|------------------------|----------|
| **Optical (Lens, Mirror)** | None acceptable | <5 μm | None on optical surfaces |
| **Medical Implant** | None acceptable | <10 μm | None on bearing surfaces |
| **Structural Ceramic** | <50 μm length | <20 μm | <5% of edge length |
| **Semiconductor** | None acceptable | <2 μm | None acceptable |

**Process Optimization:**

**Parameter Optimization Methodology:**

1. **Baseline Parameters:** Start with material-specific recommendations
2. **One-Factor-at-a-Time:** Vary frequency, amplitude, feed, or abrasive independently
3. **Measure Results:** MRR, surface finish, tool wear, edge quality
4. **Design of Experiments (DOE):** For production, use DOE to optimize multiple factors
5. **Validate:** Run confirmation trials, establish process control limits

**Example Optimization: Drilling Glass**

**Objective:** Maximize MRR while maintaining <16 μin Ra surface finish

| Trial | Frequency (kHz) | Amplitude (μm) | Abrasive Grit | MRR (mm³/min) | Surface Finish (μin Ra) | Acceptable? |
|-------|----------------|----------------|---------------|---------------|------------------------|-------------|
| **1** | 20 | 30 | 240 | 3.2 | 18 | No (finish) |
| **2** | 25 | 30 | 240 | 4.1 | 16 | Yes |
| **3** | 25 | 40 | 240 | 5.8 | 20 | No (finish) |
| **4** | 25 | 30 | 320 | 3.5 | 12 | Yes |
| **5** | 25 | 35 | 320 | 4.6 | 14 | Yes ✓ Best |

**Conclusion:** 25 kHz, 35 μm amplitude, 320 grit = best combination (4.6 mm³/min, 14 μin Ra)

### Break-Even Analysis

**Example: Drilling Holes in Zirconia Ceramic**

**Part Requirement:**
- Material: Zirconia (Y-TZP)
- Feature: (4) holes, 0.250" diameter, 0.500" deep per part
- Volume: 500 parts per year

**Option 1: Conventional Diamond Drilling (No Ultrasonic)**
- Cycle time: 12 minutes per hole × 4 holes = 48 minutes per part
- Labor rate: $45/hour
- Tool cost: $2.00 per hole × 4 = $8.00 per part
- Labor cost: (48/60) × $45 = $36.00 per part
- **Total cost per part: $44.00**
- **Annual cost (500 parts): $22,000**
- Equipment cost: $15,000 (drill press + diamond drills)

**Option 2: RUM**
- Cycle time: 3 minutes per hole × 4 holes = 12 minutes per part
- Labor rate: $50/hour (higher skill)
- Tool cost: $0.75 per hole × 4 = $3.00 per part
- Labor cost: (12/60) × $50 = $10.00 per part
- **Total cost per part: $13.00**
- **Annual cost (500 parts): $6,500**
- **Annual savings: $15,500**
- Equipment cost: $180,000 (RUM system)

**Payback Period:**
- Incremental investment: $180,000 - $15,000 = $165,000
- Annual savings: $15,500
- **Payback: 165,000 / 15,500 = 10.6 years**

**Conclusion:** At 500 parts/year, payback is marginal. Consider RUM if:
- Volume increases to 1500+/year (payback <4 years)
- Other ceramic parts can utilize same equipment
- Competitive advantage justifies investment (faster delivery, better quality)
